Choo Hiap Oh, National University of Singapore, Singapore

Title:Robust Quantum Metrological Scheme

Abstract Fragile quantum features such as entanglement have been employed to improve the precision of parameter estimation, thus inevitably the quantum gain is vulnerable to noises. As an established tool to subdue noises, quantum error correction is unfortunately overprotective because the quantum enhancement can still be achieved even if the states are unrecoverably affected, provided that the quantum Fisher information, which sets the ultimate limit to the precision of metrological schemes, is preserved and attained. Here, we shall present a robust metrological scheme that can preserve the quantum Fisher information instead of the quantum states themselves against noises. After deriving a minimal set of testable conditions on this kind of robustness, we construct a family of 2t + 1 qubits metrological schemes that are immune to t-qubit errors. In comparison at least five qubits are required for correcting arbitrary 1-qubit errors in standard quantum error correction.

Yuanping Feng, National University of Singapore, Singapore

Title: Graphene based spintronic device: enhancing spin injection efficiency

Abstract Graphene has properties attractive for spintronics applications and various graphene based spintronic devices have been proposed. However, many issues still remaining and one of them is the low efficiency of spin-injection from a metal lead to graphene. Using first-principles calculations, we investigated the efficiency of spin injection from a ferromagnetic electrode (Ni) into graphene and a possible enhancement by using a barrier between the electrode and graphene. Three types of barriers, h-BN, Cu(111), and graphite, of various thickness (0-3 layers) were considered, and the electrically biased conductance of the Ni/barrier /graphene junction was calculated. It was found that the minority-spin-transport channel of graphene can be strongly suppressed by the insulating h-BN barrier, resulting in a high spin-injection efficiency. On the other hand, the calculated spin-injection efficiency of Ni/Cu/graphene or Ni/graphite/graphene junctions is low, due to the spin-conductance mismatch. Further examination of the electronic structure of the system revealed that the high spin-injection efficiency in the presence of a tunnel barrier is due to its asymmetric effects on the two spin states of graphene.

Jiansheng Wang, National University of Singapore, Singapore

Title: Isn��t there a negative absolute temperature?

Abstract In 1956, Ramsey, based on experimental evidence of nuclear spin, developed a theory of negative temperature. The concept is challenged recently by Dunkel and Hilbert [Nature Physics 10, 67 (2014)] and others. In this talk, we review what thermodynamics is and present our support that negative temperature is a valid concept in thermodynamics and statistical mechanics.

Chorng Haur Sow, National University of Singapore, Singapore

Title: A Focused Laser Beam: Useful Tool for Nanoscience Research

Abstract Nanoscale materials have attracted great interests in recent years. Low dimensional such as nanoparticles, nanowires, nanorods, nanowalls, nanosheets, and nanojunctions or networks are an important category of nanostructured materials with great potential as important components for nanoscale devices with various interesting functions. Thus, in the past decade, many techniques have been developed for the synthesis of such nanostructured materials. In addition, researchers have put in great effort in the studies of hybrid nanomaterials. With these efforts, a wide variety of nanostructured materials have been investigated. These include carbon nanotubes, binary and ternary II-VI alloys, 2D Materials, hierarchical metal oxide and etc. After the synthesis of the nanomaterials, it is desirable to be able to further modify the properties of the nanomaterials to improve the functionality of these nanomaterials. If we can create micropatterns on these as-grown nanomaterials, it will be able to further expand their potential applications. In recent years, efforts involve the alteration of physical and/or chemical properties of nanomaterials via the use of a focused laser beam have generated a lot of interests. In this presentation, we will present our efforts in the studies of hybrid nanomaterials with the emphasis on how the focused laser beam can be used as/for (a) Micro Manipulation Tool: Optical Tweezers , (b) Micropatterning and Microstructuring Tool, (c) Micro-Architecturing, (d) Micro-Photocurrent Studies, (e) Micro Photochemical Reaction (f) Micro light-House and (g) Micro-Actuating Tool.

Mukherjee Manas, National University of Singapore, Singapore

Title: High energy physics with low energy atom �C precision measurement with trapped ions

Abstract Probing the Standard Model (SM) of particle physics is generally associated with high energy accelerator based experiments. However, till date the best test of the field theory has been through atomic physics experiments. In a similar spirit there are proposals to address the eletro-weak interaction via atomic physics experiments and one of the most successful experiments has been the observation of the parity non-conservation (PNC) in atomic Cs. That measurement was performed with a precision of 0.3% which can barely distinguish between different extensions of the SM. In this presentation, another candidate for PNC measurement will be discussed along with some preliminary results. The experiment is based on barium ion trapped and laser cooled in a linear Paul trap. A detail description of the method, setup and analysis including systematic will be discussed. In addition further discussion will be made on other ongoing experiments related to quantum information processing with the same setup.

Xuesen Wang, National University of Singapore, Singapore

Title: Introduction to the Applications of Plasmonic Metal-semiconductor Hybrid Nanostructures in Solar Energy Harvesting

Abstract Efficient and extensive harvesting of solar energy is essential to solving energy and environmental crises. Photovoltaics and photocatalysis are two potentially most important means of harvesting solar energy. Sunlight can be absorbed by semiconductors to generate hot electrons and holes, subsequently generating photocurrent in photovoltaics, or, in photocatalysis, driving desirable uphill chemical reactions such as producing hydrogen from water splitting, CO2 conversion to useful hydrocarbons, and degrading organic pollutants. The interaction of semiconductor with sunlight is rather weak, which severely limits solar energy harvesting efficiency. Au and Ag nanoparticles (NPs) exhibit strong localized surface plasmon resonance (LSPR) effect, in which the photonic field around NP surface is enhanced dramatically comparing with the far field. Such photonic field concentration leads to light trapping cross-sections much greater than semiconductor NPs, nanowires and organic dyes. LSPR can also result in hot carrier generation and local heating. When integrating plasmonic NPs with semiconductors, LSPR can significantly improve the efficiency of solar energy trapping and conversion. In thin-film solar cells, the strong localization and scattering of incident light by integrating plasmonic NPs can significantly increase light absorption by the semiconductor, thus raise cell efficiency. In photocatalysis, the LSPR of metal NPs can generate hot carriers and inject them into the semiconductor, or enhance the local light field in the adjacent semiconductor which also can yield more electrons and/or holes. Both processes can dramatically promote desirable chemical reactions. I will be briefly review the fundamental physical processes involved plasmonic-based photovoltaics and photocatalysis, and present our preliminary research results.

Zhisong Wang, National University of Singapore, Singapore

Title: Biomimicking nanomotors: a new frontier of science and technology

Abstract Molecular motors from biology and their rich variety of functionalities are a major source of inspiration for nanotechnology. In this talk, I shall first review latest experimental developments on bioinspired nanomotors. Then I shall introduce track-walking nanomotors developed in Molecular Motors Lab at NUS that capture major features of known biological nanomotors. I shall also discuss some new scientific aspects emerging from nanomotor studies, especially possible limitations of the 2nd law of thermodynamics on nanomotors although they typically operate on a non-heat source of energy in an isothermal environment.

Ling Alex, National University of Singapore, Singapore

Title: "Quantum photonics with CubeSats: A true story!"

Abstract Efforts to achieve entanglement distribution over truly long distances have generally run into limitations related to the transmission loss of optical fiber. In free-space experiments, efforts have been confounded by the need for line-of-sight, ultimately hindered by the curvature of the Earth. There have been many proposals for a space-based quantum optics platform to overcome these difficulties. At the Centre for Quantum Technologies, we believe a robust and high performance entangled photon source (with associated detection circuits) is a key enabling technology in this effort. Our system has recently reached a high level of technical maturity, with successful near-space demonstrations in 2012 and 2013, and an almost-launch in 2014. By making use of an emerging nanosatellite platform known as a CubeSat, we aim now to demonstrate the autonomous operation of entangled photon sources in low Earth orbit, ultimately pushing through existing distance limitations and facilitating the establishment of a global quantum key distribution network.

Title: Advanced Nanofunctional Materials for Li-ion Batteries

Abstract Presently used commercial lithium ion batteries (LIBs) consist of layer-type lithium cobalt oxide, spinel LiMn2O4 or LiFePO4 as the cathode, graphite as the anode material, and a non-aqueous Li - ion conducting solution or immobilized gel-polymer as an electrolyte1. LIBs are extensively used in the present-day portable electronic devices and high-power applications like back-up power supplies and electric/hybrid electric vehicles. However, LIBs need to satisfy several additional criteria, namely, cost-reduction, improvement in the energy density, safety-in-operation at high current charge/discharge rates and improvement in the low-temperature-operation.
    Several simple and complex oxide nanomaterials are synthesized by molten salt and graphenenothermal reduction methods.  These materials are characterized by Rietveld refinement X-ray diffraction, X-ray absorption fine structure, X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy, SEM, TEM, density and BET surface area methods, cyclic voltammetry, galvanostatic cycling and electrochemical impedance spectroscopy techniques. Results based on the study of binary metal oxides like MO (M= Co, Ni, Fe, Mn and Cu) and nano SnO2 and ternary oxides Nano-MCo2O4 (M= Mn, Cu, Mg, Zn) will be discussed. Advantages of nano size and matrix elements on capacity values, average charge-discharge voltages, voltage hysteresis and Electrochemical performances will be highlighted.

Qinghai Wang, National University of Singapore, Singapore

Title: Stabilizing Non-Hermitian Systems by Periodic Driving

Abstract The time evolution of a system with a time-dependent non-Hermitian Hamiltonian is in general unstable with exponential growth or decay. A periodic driving field may stabilize the dynamics because the eigenphases of the associated Floquet operator may become all real. This possibility can emerge for a continuous range of system parameters with subtle domain boundaries. It is further shown that the issue of stability of a driven non-Hermitian Rabi model can be mapped onto the band structure problem of a class of lattice Hamiltonians. As an application, we show how to use the stability of driven non-Hermitian two-level systems to simulate a spectrum analogous to Hofstadter��s butterfly that has played a paradigmatic role in quantum Hall physics.

Zexiang Shen, Nanyang Technological University��Singapore

Title: Interlayer coupling and layer-dependent electronic structure in 2D materials

Abstract Following the extensive research work on graphene, a lot of attention has now been focused on two dimensional transition metal dichalcogenide (2D TMD) materials which can in principle compensate some of the disadvantages of graphene, such as lack of a energy bandgap. 2D TMD often show very strong layer-dependent properties. For example, their properties can be strongly influenced by the stacking of the layers, the relative orientation of the layers and the number of layers. Detailed understanding of the inter-layer interaction will help greatly in tailoring the properties of 2D TMD materials for applications. Raman/Photoluminescence (PL) spectroscopy and imaging have been extensively used in the study of nano-materials and nano-devices. They provide critical information for the characterization of the materials such as electronic structure, optical property, phonon structure, defects, doping and stacking sequence. In this talk, we use Raman and PL techniques to study few-layer MoS2 samples. The Raman and PL spectra show clear correlation with layer-thickness and stacking sequence. Our ab initio calculations reveal that difference in the electronic structures mainly arises from competition between spin-orbit coupling and interlayer coupling in different structural configurations.

Shuyan Xu, Nanyang Technological University��Singapore

Title: Nonequilibrium High-density Plasma Atomisation Process for Silicon     Photovoltaics

Abstract Today��s optoelectronic and photovoltaic devices rely on the use of compound semiconductors and dielectrics. Metal organic chemical vapour deposition (MOCVD) is the most commonly used method for production of these materials. This process, however, suffers from high fabrication costs and environmental damage. Conversion of the presently dominant MOCVD manufacturing technology into cost-effective and environment-friendly technologies is a vital demand of a sustainable development of the optoelectronic and PV industry. This talk aims at developing a green and toxic-precursor free Plasma Atomized Process (PAP) technology that is capable of fabricating high-quality compound semiconductors and dielectrics without using any toxic precursors. The novel PAP concept works on the ballistic ion impact and low-energy, high-rate production of atomic radicals in high-density, nonequilibrium inductively coupled plasmas. The PAP utilizes high density plasma to create ultra-thin sheath surrounding the multiple atom clusters, leading to ballistic impact of ions onto the cluster surfaces. In-situ real-time diagnostics capture the electron information from the plasma according to the electron energy probability function (EEPF) to arrive at the ion impact information based on the ion transport mechanism across the plasma sheath. This technique is able to eliminate the use of hazardous and flammable chemicals such as silane, trimethylaluminum (TMA) and trimethylgallium (TMG). As an example, synthesis of silicon nitride and aluminium nitride and aluminium oxide for surface passivation and light management in high-efficiency silicon solar cells are demonstrated and discussed.

Title: Coordination-resolved electron spectrometrics

Abstract Probing the atomistic, dynamic, local, and quantitative information regarding bond length, bond energy, charge entrapment and polarization, energy density and cohesive energy relaxation associated with irregularly coordinated atoms has long been challenge to the community of Surface science and Nanoscience. This presentation shares an original set of theoretical, numerical, and experimental strategies that has enabled clarification, correlation, formulation, and quantification of the following:
(i)Energy levels of an isolated atom and their bulk shifts;
(ii)Acceptor-type catalysts of Pt adatoms and Cu/Pd interface and donor-type catalysts of Rh adatoms and Ag/Pd alloy;
(iii)Edge-discriminative generation of graphitic Dirac-Fermi polarons (figure);
(iv)Thermo-nuclear radiation protection ability of Be/W alloy;
(v)Size dependence of known bulk properties and size emergence of anomalies for nanocrystals.
Complementing scanning tunneling microscopy/spectroscopy and photoelectron spectroscopy, this set of approaches can monitor the energetic, electronic, and geometric behavior of irregularly coordinated atoms under external stimuli.
